02 · Intent Taxonomy

What a tutor turn actually does

Before designing a dialog, I defined the six things a tutor turn can do. Every generated response is tagged with one of these intents; every intent has its own prompt shape, its own voice persona, and its own fallback.

Elicit

Open a turn. Ask a CEFR-targeted question the learner is ready to answer in the target language.

Assess

Score the learner's production on keywords, similarity, length, and grammar. Return a structured rubric, not a raw STT confidence.

Correct

Offer a targeted fix — phonetic, lexical, or grammatical — without breaking flow or pride. Style depends on emotion signal.

Encourage

Keep the learner in the 75% flow zone. Praise is earned and specific — not a participation trophy.

Re-scaffold

Learner is below flow zone. Drop difficulty, simplify the prompt, model the target utterance, retry.

Escalate

Repeated failure or emotional drop. Switch persona to patient, lower the stakes, offer a break, flag for human tutor review.

These six intents are the conversation's grammar. An adult tutor turn in Business English B1 looks very different from a kids' turn in Spanish A2 — but they compose from the same six verbs.

03 · Turn Architecture

From utterance to response in one turn

Each learner turn is processed through a deterministic pipeline. The LLM is only one station in the pipeline, and it never speaks without rubric evidence behind it.

A single learner turn. The LLM speaks only after deterministic evaluation and intent routing have fixed the rubric.

Three things matter about this pipeline. First, the LLM never grades in a vacuum — it receives the structured evaluation output and generates a turn aligned to that rubric. Second, the intent router is deterministic: it picks correct or encourage or re-scaffold from the score distribution before the LLM ever sees the prompt. Third, the persona is selected independently from the intent, so a correct turn can be delivered gently, analytically, or patiently depending on the learner's current emotional state.

05 · Speech Evaluation

Three layers, one rubric

Acoustic STT confidence is a poor proxy for pedagogical correctness — a learner can mumble a perfect answer, or say the wrong thing clearly. Talky's cascade separates did they say anything from did they say the right thing.

L1 — Deterministic key-matching

Each curriculum unit declares its key structural phrases (e.g. "thanks for…", "based on…"). Literal keys are hit or missed. Fast, cheap, never wrong about what it measures.

L2 — Local grammar + keyword analysis

Lightweight parser flags missing articles, wrong prepositions, tense mismatches. Counts domain keywords relative to unit vocabulary. No network call.

L3 — AI judge with rubric injection

Only invoked when L1/L2 are ambiguous. The LLM is handed the rubric and the utterance; it returns a composite score: keys 50% · similarity 30% · length 10% · grammar 10%, with hard caps on meaning errors (a semantic flip — "I will" vs "I won't" — drops the score regardless of how good the grammar looked).

Alignment over accuracy

The goal isn't a maximally accurate score. It's a score aligned with teacher judgment — one an experienced instructor would defend if audited. Every rubric comes from classroom recordings of real lessons at Ciudad Bilingüe.

06 · Persona & Emotion

The same correction, three ways

The same intent delivered differently is a different turn. Talky tracks motivation, confidence, and frustration as independent signals and routes the generated response through one of three tutor personas.

Scenario
Learner missed a preposition. Intent: CORRECT. All three deliveries are equally valid.
Encouraging
Almost — tiny tweak. "on" instead of "in" there. You've been getting these all morning, let's keep rolling.
Analytical
Preposition slip: "based on" is fixed — on attaches to the reasoning. "based in" would mean physical location. Say it once more cleanly?
Patient
No stress. It's just on, not in. That's the only thing to change. Take your time.

Persona selection is independent of intent. The same correction reaches an adult interview-prep learner and a seven-year-old kid through totally different voices — but through the same architecture. That's the reusability story.

07 · Scale & Surfaces

One conversation model, five surfaces

Mobile app, live classroom, AR (for kids), 1:1 video tutoring, and an ambient ESP32 voice device (TutorPal). The intent taxonomy and the evaluation cascade are shared across all five — what changes is the turn's shape.

  • Mobile. Text + voice input, multi-turn, full rubric visible on demand. The default surface.
  • Classroom (50 students live). The tutor turn becomes a broadcast; learner responses are batched and scored in parallel; persona shifts to analytical so the public feedback generalises.
  • AR Kids. The tutor is an animated character anchored in the room. Intents stay; latency budget is tighter; personas bias toward encouraging and patient.
  • Video 1:1. The tutor is a human; the AI is relegated to real-time captioning, on-screen rubric hints, and post-session summary.
  • TutorPal (ambient ESP32). No screen. Turn is audio-only, shorter, and lowered-stakes — a "quick drill" mode aimed at incidental exposure, not progression.

Design principle

Every surface is a different physical context but the same conversation spine. Intents and evaluation are shared; only the turn length, pacing, and modality change. This is what lets a 5-person team ship for 9 languages across 5 form factors.

08 · Relevance

What I'd bring to a customer-support virtual-agent team

A Netflix member hitting the virtual agent at 11pm with a billing problem is, structurally, a learner hitting Talky — a human in a high-stakes multi-turn conversation with an AI that needs to route intent, track emotion, maintain persona, and know when to escalate. The surface is different; the craft is the same.

  • Intent design, not prompt hacking. I think in turn-taxonomies and rubrics, then write the prompts to match. Prompt iteration without a rubric is noise.
  • Emotion as a first-class signal. Detecting when a member is escalating, retreating, or disengaging — and switching persona rather than script — is the difference between a helpful agent and a frustrating one.
  • Reusable, multilingual frameworks. Shipping the same conversation grammar across 9 languages teaches you which patterns generalise, which need localisation, and which need to be re-thought from the ground up per locale.
  • Voice and chat parity. The same intent must read well as text and sound right as speech — the evaluation and generation pipelines need to be indifferent to surface.
  • LLM-grounded, rule-reinforced. I know where LLMs shine (style, recovery, nuance) and where they fail (high-stakes determinism) — and how to compose the two so an enterprise agent is never just "whatever the model said this time."
